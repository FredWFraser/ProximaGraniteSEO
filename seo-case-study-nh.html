How I Grew My SEO Website from Zero to Visibility in New Hampshire's Local Market


The Challenge

When I launched Proxima Granite SEO, I had no domain authority, no backlinks, and no search presence — just a blank slate and a commitment to doing SEO the right way.

I set out on my journey to launch my own business after a career in software quality assurance. I wanted something new but still technical and analytical. One day, I came across a course on Full Stack Web Development and thought: maybe this is something I could do from home. That led me to SEO. I took a comprehensive SEO course and went from knowing nothing to feeling confident in my abilities. Then I found a course on how to start an SEO business. I followed that with WordPress website development training — and then I decided it was time to stop learning and start doing.

I started my business, got an EIN, bought a domain name, and began writing my website. The basic site took about a day and a quarter to complete. I pushed the code to GitHub for free hosting — and just like that, my website was live. I was proud of how it looked, especially the gradient background. Everything seemed to be coming together… except for one thing: I wasn't ranking. I couldn't even find my site when I searched for my business name.

The Strategy

It was time to put my SEO skills to the test. I revisited the course I had completed, this time with a live site to work on. I began with keyword research and targeted local terms like “New Hampshire SEO Company”, which, according to my research, gets between 100 and 1,000 monthly searches.

I applied the fundamentals of on-page SEO — placing keywords thoughtfully across pages like the homepage, services, FAQs, about, and contact. I made sure every page was linked internally, avoiding orphan pages.

Next came technical SEO: improving site speed, mobile responsiveness, and adding schema markup that search engines understand but users don’t see. For local optimization, I set up my Google Business Profile and claimed my Yelp listing. I also made content improvements, like writing FAQs, optimizing meta titles and descriptions, adding alt text to images, and uploading a favicon so my logo would appear in browser tabs.

I even added structured data to help my logo appear in search results, though Google ultimately decides whether to display it.

The Results

At first, even a search for “Proxima Granite SEO” didn't bring up my site — a common hurdle for new websites. I submitted my pages to Google Search Console to speed up indexing (optional, but helpful). Soon, my website began to appear. That moment — seeing my business in Google — was exciting and validating.

I showed up on Google Maps. I ranked at or near the top of the first page for my business name. Within a few days, I began ranking for terms like:

  • SEO NH
  • SEO near me
  • SEO Rockingham County NH

Traffic began trickling in, and I saw my performance in Search Console getting stronger. For a brand-new website, that's a big deal. SEO is typically a long game, and results often take months. But the right foundation can accelerate progress.

I created this capstone page not just to tell my story — but to strengthen my SEO even more. I considered writing a blog, but for now, this “living” case study will serve as both proof of concept and a place for future updates.

What This Means for Clients

The exact strategies I used for my own business can be used to grow yours — especially if you're a small business in New Hampshire or a service provider in a competitive local market.

SEO isn't predictable. Even though I was ranking for “NH SEO” — jumping from page 9 to page 4 — I later dropped out of the rankings entirely. Google's algorithms are always changing. The key isn't perfection — it's persistence, testing, and staying connected to what's working in the SEO world.

Through this process, I've come to see SEO as similar to a medical practice. There are proven methods, but not every tactic works for every “patient.” You try, observe, adjust — and try again. The goal is always to help, never to harm. And when something doesn't work, you keep going until you find what does.

Final Thoughts

SEO isn't exact. It's guided by best practices, shaped by ever-changing algorithms, and influenced by unique business goals. Like medicine, it's part science, part art — and it takes time.
